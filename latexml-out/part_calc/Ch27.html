27.1 Motivation

Before really telling you what ‚Ñ§p and ‚Ñöp are, let me tell you what you might expect them to do.

In elementary/olympiad number theory, we‚Äôre already well-familiar with the following two ideas:

  • ‚Ä¢

  • ‚Ä¢

Let me expand on the first point. Suppose we have some Diophantine equation. In olympiad contexts, one can take an equation modulo p to gain something else to work with. Unfortunately, taking modulo p loses some information: the reduction ‚Ñ§‚Ü†‚Ñ§/p is far from injective.

This can also give some new information (cubes modulo 9, anyone?), but it has the disadvantage that ‚Ñ§/p2 isn‚Äôt a field, so we lose a lot of the nice algebraic properties that we got if we take modulo p.

One of the goals of p-adic numbers is that we can get around these two issues I described. The p-adic numbers we introduce is going to have the following properties:

  1. 1.

    You can ‚Äútake modulo pe for all e at once‚Äù. In olympiad contexts, we are used to picking a particular modulus and then seeing what happens if we take that modulus. But with p-adic numbers, we won‚Äôt have to make that choice. An equation of p-adic numbers carries enough information to take modulo pe.

  2. 2.

    The numbers ‚Ñöp form a field, the nicest possible algebraic structure: 1/p makes sense. Contrast this with ‚Ñ§/p2, which is not even an integral domain.

  3. 3.

    It doesn‚Äôt lose as much information as taking modulo p does: rather than the surjective ‚Ñ§‚Ü†‚Ñ§/p we have an injective map ‚Ñ§‚Ü™‚Ñ§p.

  4. 4.

    Despite this, you ‚Äúignore‚Äù some ‚Äúirrelevant‚Äù data.
