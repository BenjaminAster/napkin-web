Chapter 51 The Riemann-Roch theorem

51.1 Motivation

Recall a basic fact in complex analysis:

A holomorphic ℂ→ℂ function is uniquely determined by its Taylor series expansion at the origin.

Compared to the case of real smooth function, this is already very rigid — the value of the function in a small neighborhood of the origin determines the value of the function everywhere — but, in order to specify a function, you still need infinitely many coordinates!

Meanwhile, we have Liouville’s theorem:

A bounded holomorphic ℂ→ℂ function is constant.

As we have learnt earlier, this theorem, when phrased in terms of Riemann surfaces, can be more elegantly rephrased to the following:

A holomorphic ℂ∞→ℂ function is constant.

In other words, in order to specify a holomorphic ℂ∞→ℂ, you only need a single complex number! That is, the ℂ-vector space Hom⁡(ℂ∞,ℂ) has dimension 1.

Naturally, you may ask, “is there anything inbetween”? There is! And the Riemann-Roch theorem is the main ingredient to understand how these things work.

So, how are we going to define this? If you compare the two situations above, a holomorphic ℂ→ℂ function is a meromorphic ℂ∞→ℂ function, which is allowed to have a pole at ∞, and nowhere else.

So,

Moral 51.1.1 —

By smoothly interpolate between “allow pole of arbitrary order” and “must be holomorphic”, we can produce many interesting spaces of functions.

Conveniently, back in chapter 32, we have defined the multiplicity of a zero and the order of a pole of a meromorphic function. So, the natural point between these two extremes is to allow a pole of order at most d.

For notational convenience, we defines:

Definition 51.1.2 (Order of a meromorphic function).

Let f be meromorphic at p. We define ordp⁡(f) to be:

  • •

    d, if f has a zero of multiplicity d at p;

  • •

    −d, if f has a pole of order d at p;

  • •

    0, otherwise.

Example 51.1.3 (The space of functions with pole of order at most 4 on ℂ∞)

Let L⁢(4⋅∞) be the set of meromorphic ℂ∞→ℂ function, being holomorphic everywhere except ∞, and has a pole of order at most 4 at ∞ — in other words,

L⁢(4⋅∞)={f meromorphic on ℂ∞∣f defined on ℂ∞∖{∞},ord∞⁡(f)≥−4}.

(The notation L⁢(−) will be explained later.)

Obviously, this forms a natural ℂ-vector space.

Consider the Taylor series of any f∈L⁢(4⋅∞) at the origin:

f⁢(z)=c−mzm+c−m+1zm−1+⋯+c−1z+c0+c1⁢z+⋯

Obviously, because f is defined at the origin, it cannot have any nonzero coefficient c−m for m>0. But more importantly, it cannot have any nonzero coefficient cm for m>4 either!111The reason is actually not very straightforward, but you can see for yourself why it is true: if there are only finitely many nonzero terms, then the order of the pole at ∞, (−ord∞⁡(f)), is precisely the degree of the highest nonzero coefficient.

Did you see what happened here? We start with requiring the function to be analytic and does not blow up too badly, and we end up with just the algebraic function — the polynomials!

In particular, L⁢(4⋅∞) consists of the polynomials of degree ≤4, and

dimL⁢(4⋅∞)=5

as a ℂ-vector space.

Example 51.1.4 (More complicated L⁢(−) spaces)

There’s no reason why we should restrict ourselves to considering only the functions that blow up at ∞ — as we will see, more general meromorphic functions can be considered, as long as we restrict the order of the poles.

Let L⁢(−1⋅3+4⋅i+5⋅∞) be the set of meromorphic functions f:ℂ∞→ℂ that are:

  • •

    holomorphic everywhere in ℂ∞, possibly with the exception of the points 3, i, and ∞;

  • •

    at 3, it must have a root of order ≥1;

  • •

    at i, it cannot have a pole of order more than 4;

  • •

    at ∞, it cannot have a pole of order more than 5.

So, for example, (z↦z−3), (z↦(z−3)3(z−i)2), or (z↦(z−3)4) are functions in the set, but not (z↦(z−3)2+1) or (z↦(z−3)7).

As before, this is a ℂ-vector space, and furthermore, it is also finite-dimensional! What should its dimension be?

Well, note that there is a 1-1 bijection between functions f∈L⁢(−1⋅3+4⋅i+3⋅∞) and functions g∈L⁢(−1⋅3+7⋅∞) by

g=Φ⁢(f)=(z↦f⁢(z)⋅(z−i)4),

where, as you could probably have guessed by now, L⁢(−1⋅3+7⋅∞) is the space of meromorphic functions that has at least a zero at 3 and at most a pole of order 7 at ∞.

Using that information, it shouldn’t be too hard for you to see that the dimension should be 7.

For another piece of motivation: Later on, we will also define the concept of divisors and line bundles. If you have learned about these concepts in algebraic geometry context, you might be interested to learn what they are actually about; otherwise, it is still very surprising that these theorems can be naturally generalized to completely algebraic settings, and your intuition from the case of analytic manifold will mostly work verbatim — in fact, you can even define the genus of a number field, like ℚ⁢[2]!

51.2 Divisors

Prototypical example for this section: (−3)⋅i+(−4)⋅∞ is a divisor on ℂ∞.

We start with defining a convenient notation for the above concepts.

First, observe that the condition “f must have a zero of order at most 4 at the origin” can be conveniently written as

z4∣f.

In other words, z4 must be a divisor of f.

This notation works if f is a polynomial, since we already know what it means for two polynomials to divide each other.

Generalizing, we could say “f cannot have a pole of order more than 3 at the point i, and f cannot have a pole of order more than 4 at the point ∞” by

(z−i)−3⋅(z−∞)−4∣f.

Of course, at this point, the notation is purely formal — there is no interpretation as “functions” that could be assigned to the expression (z−∞), for instance.

Those objects are, appropriately enough, called divisors. So we come to the formal definition:

Definition 51.2.1 (Divisors).

Let X be a Riemann manifold, then a divisor D on X is a function D:X→ℤ, which is nonzero on a discrete set of points.

The formal objects (z−i)−3⋅(z−∞)−4 above, from now on, we will consider it as a function D:ℂ∞→ℤ by

D⁢(z)={−3z=i−4z=∞0otherwise.
Abuse of Notation 51.2.2.

For a point p∈X, we write p to mean the divisor that takes value 1 at p, and 0 elsewhere.

Because divisors are integer-valued functions, we can add two divisors together or multiply a divisor with an integer, the result is an integer. So,

Example 51.2.3 ((z−i)−3⋅(z−∞)−4 as a divisor)

The divisor D that corresponds to the formal object (z−i)−3⋅(z−∞)−4 above can be written as (−3)⋅i+(−4)⋅∞.

51.3 Degree of a divisor

Prototypical example for this section: deg⁡((−3)⋅i+(−4)⋅∞)=−7.

If the surface X is compact, any discrete set of points is finite. Thus, a divisor D on X has finite support.

This allows us to define the degree of a divisor:

Definition 51.3.1 (Degree of a divisor).

For a divisor D on a compact surface X, its degree is ∑p∈XD⁢(p).

Of course, the sum is well-defined because only finitely many terms are nonzero.

51.4 The principal divisor of a meromorphic function

Prototypical example for this section: div⁡(z−3)2z−i=2⋅3+(−1)⋅i+(−1)⋅∞ has degree 0.

After defining a divisor, we want a convenient notation to formalize our fuzzy notation earlier of a divisor “divides” a function.

Definition 51.4.1 (Divisor of a meromorphic function).

Let f be meromorphic on a Riemann surface X. Then the divisor of f, div⁡(f), is such that

div⁡(f)⁢(p)=ordp⁡(f).

We can also write it as a formal sum: div⁡(f)=∑pordp⁡(f)⋅p — by the abuse of notation above, this would be an actual sum if f has finitely many roots and poles.

If a divisor D is equal to div⁡(f) for some f, we call D a principal divisor. (Compare this with a principal ideal, being an ideal generated by one element!)

Looking at the prototype example of this section, you might have guessed the following for the Riemann sphere. In fact, much more is true:

Proposition 51.4.2

If a divisor D on a compact surface X is principal, then deg⁡D=0.

Let us not forget our goal of defining a convenient notation to talk about the space of functions with bounded poles. With the notation defined above, if f=z, div⁡f=1⋅0+(−1)⋅∞, and we want to say f “divides” the divisor (−1)⋅∞. The natural definition would be:

Definition 51.4.3 (The partial ordering of divisors).

We write D≥0 if D⁢(p)≥0 for all p∈X.

For two divisors D1 and D2, we write D1≥D2 if D1−D2≥0.

And finally,

Definition 51.4.4.

Let D be a divisor on a Riemann surface X. Then the space of meromorphic functions with poles bounded by D is

L⁢(D)={f meromorphic on X∣div⁡(f)≥−D}.
Exercise 51.4.5

This exercise is just for you to get familiar with the notation. Show the following:

  • •

    For two divisors D1≤D2, then L⁢(D1)⊆L⁢(D2).

  • •

    If X is compact, then L⁢(0)≅ℂ.

  • •

    If X is compact and deg⁡D<0, then L⁢(D)={0}.

Theorem 51.5.1 (The Riemann-Roch theorem)

dimL⁢(D)−dimL⁢(K−D)=deg⁡(D)+1−g.
